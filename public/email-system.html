Section 2

Email Cadence Framework

Monday: Educational Deep Dive

Role: Build authority. Position Ben as the educator who makes complex things simple.

Psychology: The reader leaves feeling smarter. They learned something they can use immediately. This creates a micro-debt - "this person taught me something valuable for free. What would the paid version look like?"

Structure:

  1. Hook (1-2 sentences): Start with a provocative question or counterintuitive statement
  2. Context (2-3 sentences): Why this matters right now
  3. Framework (core of email): Teach one concept deeply. Use numbered lists, specific data, real examples
  4. Takeaway (1 sentence): What the reader should do right now (30-second action)
  5. CTA: Blueprint Assessment or Starter Kit - always educational framing, never salesy

Content pillars rotation: Strategy (Week 1) - Confidence (Week 2) - Protect (Week 3) - Results (Week 4)

Wednesday: Story + Belief Break

Role: Shift a limiting belief that keeps the reader stuck. Use story to bypass intellectual resistance.

Psychology: Facts tell, stories sell. When someone reads about Leslie (62, investment banker) who thought she was "too late" - and sees herself in that story - the belief cracks. Stories bypass the rational brain and speak to identity. "If she did it, maybe I can too."

Emotional Arc:

  1. Personal hook: Ben connects emotionally first (vulnerability from his own journey)
  2. Introduce the character: A real member with a relatable situation
  3. The belief that held them back: Name it explicitly - "too late," "too risky," "I'm not technical enough"
  4. The shift: What changed. Not the product - the realization
  5. The result: Where they are now. Specific, not vague
  6. The belief break: One paragraph that reframes the belief for the reader
  7. CTA: Assessment + optional strategy call mention in P.S.

Friday: Open Loop into VSL

Role: Create curiosity that can only be satisfied by watching the free training (VSL V5).

Psychology: The Zeigarnik Effect - the brain remembers uncompleted tasks better than completed ones. Open a loop (tease a chart, framework, or insight) but don't close it. The only way to close the loop is to watch the training.

Curiosity Mechanics:

  1. Set the scene: Ben in a specific situation (presenting to investors, reviewing a portfolio, on a call)
  2. Tease the insight: "I showed them one chart" or "I asked them three questions" - be specific enough to intrigue, vague enough to not complete
  3. Create the gap: "The room went quiet" or "Nobody could answer the third question"
  4. Bridge to VSL: "I recorded a free training that walks through the whole thing"
  5. CTA: Watch the free training - collectiveshift.net/trainingvslv5

Critical rule: Never complete the loop in the email. The email's job is to create desire to watch - not to teach.

The Weekly Narrative Arc

The three emails aren't isolated. They build:

  • Monday: "Here's something you didn't know" (competence gap revealed)
  • Wednesday: "Here's someone like you who figured it out" (possibility demonstrated)
  • Friday: "Here's how to figure it out yourself" (solution teased, VSL delivers)

By Friday, the reader has been shown a gap in their knowledge, shown proof that the gap can be closed, and given a free path to close it. That's a complete persuasion sequence in three touchpoints.

When to Break the Pattern

  • Market crash / major move: Skip the cadence, send a timely "Here's what's happening and what we're doing" email within 4 hours
  • Product launch: Add a Thursday email for launch week only. Never more than 4 emails in a week.
  • Live event promotion: Replace Friday open-loop with event CTA, 2 weeks before event
  • Holiday weeks: Send Monday only. Respect the ICP's time.
  • Weekend: Never send on weekends. Our ICP is spending time with family.

A/B: The boring strategy that outperforms almost everything

Every week someone asks me the same question: "Should I put it all in now or spread it out over time?" It's a good question. And the answer might surprise you. Most financial media will tell you "lump sum wins 2 out of 3 times" based on studies of the stock market. That's true for equities. But digital assets aren't equities. And the data tells a different story when volatility is higher. Here's what we've seen across 6 years of managing portfolios: The investors who DCA'd into Bitcoin between 2019 and 2025 had a smoother ride and, critically, were far more likely to stick with their strategy during the 40-60% drawdowns that happen in this asset class. The lump sum investors? Some did better on pure returns. But a meaningful percentage panic-sold during the 2022 bear market because they were "all in" and watching their entire allocation drop by half. The real question isn't "which performs better mathematically." It's "which will I actually stick with?" Here's the framework we use with our members: Use DCA when: - You're deploying more than 5% of your net worth into digital assets - You're new and still building conviction - You have ongoing income you can allocate monthly - You get anxious watching price movements Consider lump sum when: - You have strong conviction in your thesis (you've done the work) - The market is in a confirmed accumulation zone (not at all-time highs) - The amount is small relative to your total portfolio - You have a written sell strategy already in place Most of our Platinum members use a hybrid: they deploy 50-60% as an initial allocation, then DCA the remaining 40-50% over 3-6 months. This gives them immediate exposure while smoothing out entry price. The takeaway: The best strategy is the one you won't abandon when the market drops 40%. For most people, that's DCA. The sell strategy most people don't know exists
A/B: Access cash from your Bitcoin without selling a single sat
Preview: One member accessed $200K without triggering a taxable event.
What if you could access the value of your Bitcoin without actually selling it? No taxable event. No capital gains hit. No losing your position. This isn't a trick. It's a financial tool that traditional wealth managers have used for decades with property and equities. It's called lending against your assets. And it works with Bitcoin. Here's how it works in plain terms: You hold Bitcoin. Instead of selling it (which triggers capital gains tax and removes your exposure to future upside), you use it as collateral for a loan. You get cash. Your Bitcoin stays in a custodial wallet as collateral. You repay the loan over time and get your Bitcoin back. The result: you accessed liquidity without selling. No taxable event. Your Bitcoin position stays intact. If Bitcoin appreciates while you hold the loan, you benefit from that upside. Why this matters for the ICP we work with: Most of our members are professionals or business owners with significant wealth. They don't need to sell Bitcoin to survive. They need to access capital strategically - for a property deposit, a business investment, or simply to rebalance without a tax bill. One member used this approach to access over $200K in cash without selling a single Bitcoin. His accountant confirmed: no taxable event. That's the difference between a strategy and guessing. The risks (because nothing is free): - If Bitcoin drops significantly, you may face a margin call (need to add more collateral or repay early) - Interest rates on these loans vary - typically 5-12% APR depending on the platform and LTV ratio - Custodial risk - your Bitcoin is held by the lending platform during the loan This is why we only recommend this strategy to members who have: (a) a portfolio large enough that the loan-to-value ratio stays conservative (below 50%), (b) a backup plan if prices drop, and (c) access to reputable, regulated lending platforms. The takeaway: Selling isn't the only exit strategy. For high-net-worth investors holding significant Bitcoin positions, lending against your assets is a tool worth understanding. It won't be right for everyone. But for the right situation, it's a game-changer. "How much of my portfolio should be in Bitcoin?" I get asked this more than any other question. And I'll be honest - there is no universal answer. Anyone who tells you "put 10% in Bitcoin" without knowing your situation is giving you a template, not a strategy. But there IS a framework for figuring it out. Here's what we use with our 1,163 members. The 4 allocation factors: 1. Total net worth and liquidity Someone with $5M in property, $500K in super, and $200K in cash has a very different risk capacity than someone with $300K total and a mortgage. The first person can allocate 10-15% to digital assets without it being an existential risk. The second person needs to be more conservative - maybe 3-5%. 2. Time horizon If you need this money in 12 months, digital assets are the wrong tool. Full stop. If your time horizon is 5+ years, you can absorb the volatility. 10+ years? You're in a position to benefit from it. 3. Existing diversification If 80% of your wealth is in Australian property (and for many of our members it is), you're already heavily concentrated in one asset class, one country, and one currency. Digital assets aren't adding risk in this context - they're adding diversification. 4. Psychological capacity This is the one nobody talks about. Can you watch a 40% drawdown and not panic? Be honest. If a 40% drop would cause you to sell, your allocation is too high. The right allocation is one that lets you sleep at night AND participate in the upside. The ranges we typically see: Conservative (Accumulator profile): 2-5% of total net worth Moderate (Strategist profile): 5-15% of total net worth Aggressive (Compounder profile): 15-30% of total net worth These aren't recommendations. They're ranges we've observed across 1,100+ members. Your number should be based on your specific situation across all four factors. The takeaway: Allocation is not a feeling. It's a calculation. If you haven't done the calculation, you're either under-allocated (missing growth) or over-allocated (one drawdown from panic-selling). Email Copy Guidelines & Templates

Subject Line Formulas (10 Proven Frameworks)

  1. The Diagnostic: "The [X] investor types (which one are you?)"
  2. Belief Challenge: "I thought [common belief]. I was wrong."
  3. Specific Number: "The [X] questions our members answer before every trade"
  4. Story Hook: "[Quote from member]" - use real quotes in quotation marks
  5. Data Point: "[Specific fact] and what it means for your portfolio"
  6. Curiosity Gap: "The chart that changed how I think about [topic]"
  7. Counterintuitive: "The boring strategy that outperforms almost everything"
  8. Direct Question: "How much Bitcoin is enough?"
  9. Personal Confession: "The mistake that cost me everything (twice)"
  10. Social Proof Tease: "What Coinbase saw in our research"

Rules: Under 50 characters when possible. No emojis. No ALL CAPS. No clickbait. Always write an A/B variant.

Open-Loop Techniques

  • The Incomplete List: "I asked them 3 questions. I'll give you the first one here..." (reader needs VSL for questions 2 and 3)
  • The Visual Tease: "I put up a chart with two lines. When the second line appeared..." (reader needs to see the chart)
  • The Room Reaction: "The room went quiet for 30 seconds." (reader wants to know what caused it)
  • The Framework Hint: "The 8-step system we use..." (reader wants the full system)
  • The Consequence: "That's the mistake that cost one member $180K." (reader wants to avoid the same mistake)

Critical rule: Never close the loop in the email. The email creates desire. The VSL delivers.

Preview Text Strategy

Preview text (50-90 characters) appears next to the subject line in inbox view. It should complement, not repeat the subject line.

  • Pattern: Subject = curiosity hook. Preview = contextual clue that increases open desire.
  • Example: Subject: "The 3 investor types" / Preview: "There's a reason we don't start with 'what to buy.'"
  • Example: Subject: "The chart that changed how I think about wealth" / Preview: "I recorded a short training that walks through it."
  • Never: Use the first line of the email as preview text. Always write it deliberately.

"Do This / Not That" for Email Copy

✓ Do This

  • Write like you're texting a smart friend
  • Use "digital assets" (not "crypto")
  • Use dashes (-) or commas for breaks
  • Include specific data: "116% CAGR, audited"
  • Use real names (anonymised): "Leslie, 62"
  • One CTA per email. One.
  • End with "Talk soon, Ben"
  • Include compliance line at bottom
  • Use short paragraphs (1-3 sentences max)
  • Reference the Shift OS by step number

✗ Not That

  • Sound like a marketing email
  • Use "crypto" (except in legal context)
  • Use em dashes ( - ) ever
  • Make vague claims: "amazing returns"
  • Use generic stories: "one of our members..."
  • Include multiple CTAs or links
  • End with "Best regards" or "Cheers"
  • Skip the disclaimer
  • Write wall-of-text paragraphs
  • Guarantee outcomes or income

Ben's Voice Checklist

  • Would Ben actually say this out loud? If not, rewrite.
  • Is the first sentence a hook, not a greeting? (No "Hi there" or "Hope you're well")
  • Are there zero em dashes in the entire email?
  • Is every data point real and sourced? (116% CAGR, 1,163 members, audited by Crypto Tax Australia)
  • Is there a personal element? (Ben's story, a member story, a specific moment)
  • Does it teach something or shift a belief? (Not just inform)
  • Is the CTA natural - not forced? (Flows from the content)
  • Would a 50-year-old surgeon with $2M in assets read this and think "this person gets me"?
  • Is it under 500 words? (250-500 sweet spot)
  • Zero hype words? (No "incredible," "amazing," "exclusive opportunity," "don't miss out")

A/B Testing Plan

Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4): Subject lines only. Test A vs B for every email. Winner = higher open rate after 2 hours.

Phase 2 (Weeks 5-8): Send times. Test 7am vs 5pm in recipient's timezone. Monday stays Monday, but time shifts.

Phase 3 (Weeks 9-12): CTA placement. Test CTA at end only vs CTA mid-email + end.

Phase 4 (Ongoing): Content format. Test story-first vs data-first openings. Test short (200 words) vs standard (400 words).

Rule: Only test one variable at a time. Only declare winners with 100+ opens per variant.

Re-engagement Sequence (Cold Subscribers)

For subscribers with no opens for 30+ days:

  1. Email 1 (Day 0): "Still interested in digital assets?" - Short, direct. One question. One link. Subject: "Quick question"
  2. Email 2 (Day 3): Best-performing email from last month (re-send to non-openers with new subject line)
  3. Email 3 (Day 7): "Last email from me unless you want to stay" - Clean break offer. Click to stay on list.
  4. No response after Email 3: Suppress from all sends. Move to "Dormant" segment. Do not delete - they may re-engage through other channels.

Why this matters: Sending to people who never open destroys deliverability. A clean list with 45% open rates beats a bloated list with 15% open rates every time.
