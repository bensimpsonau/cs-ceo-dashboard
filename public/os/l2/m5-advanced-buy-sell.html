TOPIC 1

DeFi Yields Explained — Where Does the Money Come From?

The single most important question in DeFi: "Where does the yield come from?" If a member can't answer this, they don't understand the risk. This topic builds that understanding from first principles.

How DeFi Yields Work: Lending, Liquidity Provision, Staking

Lending: you deposit, borrowers pay interest. You earn a share of that interest. Liquidity provision: you provide trading pairs to a DEX, earning trading fees. Staking: you lock tokens to secure a network, earning newly minted tokens + transaction fees. Each mechanism has a different risk profile — teach all three.

Sustainable vs Unsustainable Yields

Sustainable: yield comes from real economic activity (trading fees, borrowing interest, network security). Unsustainable: yield comes from token emissions (printing new tokens to pay holders — the yield is literally inflation). The Ponzi test: if new users stopped joining, would the yield still exist? If no → unsustainable.

⭐ The 4-Tier Yield Spectrum — Core Framework

This is the signature L2 framework for evaluating yield opportunities. Every yield product fits somewhere on this spectrum. Teach members to identify which tier they're in before committing capital.

TIER 1 2–5% APY — Treasury-Backed, Lowest Risk

Examples: Coinbase USDC earn, Binance Earn flexible savings, traditional CeFi platforms. Risk profile: counterparty risk (exchange), regulatory risk. Comparable to: traditional term deposits. Who it's for: Wealth Protection members, new L2 members starting with yield.

TIER 2 5–9% APY — Established DeFi Protocols, Moderate Risk

Examples: Aave (lending), Compound (lending), established liquid staking (Lido). Risk profile: smart contract risk + counterparty risk. Requires: understanding of protocol mechanics, wallet interaction. Who it's for: L2 members comfortable with on-chain transactions.

TIER 3 9–18% APY — Institutional DeFi ← Where CS Sits

Examples: Maple Finance/syrupUSDC (CS position @ 33.4%), institutional credit protocols. Risk profile: credit risk (borrower default) + smart contract risk. Requires: understanding of credit markets, institutional due diligence. CS rationale: real borrowers (institutions), audited, transparent, higher yield compensates for higher risk. Who it's for: experienced L2 members with strong risk understanding.

TIER 4 18%+ APY — Higher Risk, Deep Due Diligence Required

Examples: Leveraged yield farming, new protocol incentives, high-risk lending pools. Risk profile: ALL of the above + impermanent loss + protocol rug risk + liquidity risk. Warning: most 18%+ yields are unsustainable or carry risks members don't fully understand. Who it's for: only the most sophisticated L2 members, tiny position sizes only.

Smart Contract Risk: What It Means and How to Evaluate

Every DeFi interaction involves smart contracts — code that holds your money. Smart contract risk = the code has a bug or exploit. How to evaluate: audit status (has it been audited? by whom?), time in production (battle-tested?), TVL (more money = more scrutiny). Major hacks: Ronin ($600M+), Wormhole ($320M+) — these are real examples of smart contract risk materialising.

Impermanent Loss Basics for Liquidity Providers

What it is: when the relative price of tokens in your LP position changes, you can end up with less value than if you'd simply held. When it matters: volatile pairs (ETH/random altcoin). When it doesn't: stable pairs (USDC/USDT). Why it's called "impermanent": if prices return to original ratio, the loss disappears. Calculator tools for estimating IL.

TOPIC 2

Stablecoin Strategies — The Bridge Between TradFi and DeFi

What Stablecoins Are and Peg Mechanisms

USDC (Circle): fully backed by cash + short-term treasuries, regulated, most transparent. USDT (Tether): largest by market cap, less transparent reserves, some controversy. DAI (MakerDAO): crypto-collateralised, decentralised, algorithmic stability. Risk comparison: USDC (lowest risk) > DAI (moderate) > USDT (higher counterparty risk).

Stablecoin Yield Platforms and Deployment

CeFi options: Coinbase USDC earn, Nexo, BlockFi (caution: CeFi platform risk). DeFi options: Aave, Compound (Tier 2), Maple/syrupUSDC (Tier 3 — CS position). How to evaluate a platform before deploying capital. Comparing stablecoin yields to traditional alternatives: term deposits (currently ~4-5% AUD), savings accounts (~5%).

TOPIC 3

Staking Fundamentals — Earning Yield on Holdings

What Staking Is: Proof of Stake Explained

Lock tokens to help secure the network. In return, earn newly minted tokens + a share of transaction fees. Available for PoS chains: Ethereum, Solana, Cosmos chains, etc. Not available for Bitcoin (PoW — different consensus mechanism).

⚠️ Staking ≠ Free Money: The Inflation Offset Reality

Critical teaching point: ETH staking ~3-4% APR, SOL ~7% APR. BUT network inflation is also creating new tokens. Staking rewards partially (or fully) offset inflation — the "real yield" may be much lower than the headline number. If you DON'T stake, you're being diluted. If you DO stake, you're maintaining your share. This directly connects to the "real yield vs nominal yield" concept from Module 3 Tokenomics.

Native Staking vs Liquid Staking

Native staking: lock tokens directly with a validator. Pros: simplest, direct. Cons: locked, can't use for other purposes. Liquid staking (e.g., Lido stETH, Jito jitoSOL): stake and receive a liquid token representing your staked position. Pros: maintain liquidity, can use in DeFi. Cons: additional smart contract risk, slight discount to underlying.

Risks: Slashing, Lock-Up Periods, Validator Risk

Slashing: if your validator misbehaves, a portion of your stake is destroyed. Lock-up periods: some chains require unstaking periods (ETH: variable, SOL: ~2-3 days). Validator risk: choosing a reliable validator matters. Which platforms to use for staking each asset.

Where to Store Cash/Stablecoins — Convenience vs Security vs Yield

On exchange: most convenient, lowest yield, counterparty risk. On-chain CeFi (e.g., Coinbase USDC earn): moderate convenience, moderate yield (Tier 1-2). DeFi protocols: highest yield potential, requires wallet interaction, smart contract risk. Hardware wallet: most secure, no yield. The tradeoff matrix — members choose based on their risk tolerance and portfolio size.
