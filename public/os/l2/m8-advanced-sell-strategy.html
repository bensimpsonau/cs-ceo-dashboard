TOPIC 1

Advanced Exit Frameworks — Beyond Targets and DCA-Out

Multi-Tier Exit Ladders

Sell 20-25% of a position at each price target across multiple levels. Example for an altcoin position: Tier 1 (2x from entry): sell 25%. Tier 2 (3x): sell 25%. Tier 3 (5x): sell 25%. Tier 4 (moonshot): let the final 25% ride with a trailing stop. Why laddering works: you never sell "too early" or "too late" — you sell systematically. Applied across entire portfolio, not just per-position.

Structuring Exits Across an Entire Portfolio

Portfolio-level exit planning: different assets have different rules. Bitcoin: very long timeframe, small trims only in extreme overheating. Altcoins: aggressive laddering — "good time, not a long time." Stablecoins: increase stablecoin allocation as cycle matures (build war chest for re-entry). How to create a portfolio-level exit matrix: asset × tier × target × action.

Stablecoin Rotation Strategy

Sell into stablecoins instead of fiat — stays in the ecosystem, ready for re-deployment. Deploy stablecoins for yield while waiting for re-entry (connects to Module 5: 4-Tier Yield Spectrum). The cycle: sell altcoins → stablecoins → earn yield → wait for correction → re-enter. Tax note: selling into stablecoins IS a CGT event (same as selling to fiat).

Options and Futures for Hedging (Awareness Only)

Brief overview: buying put options to protect downside on a large Bitcoin position. Not a how-to — awareness level only. Where this exists: Deribit, CME futures. Why most members shouldn't use these: complexity, margin risk, cost of premiums. When it might make sense: $500K+ Bitcoin positions where hedging cost is justified.

TOPIC 2

Portfolio Rebalancing — Systematic Portfolio Management

When to Rebalance

Time-based: quarterly review. Threshold-based: when any position drifts >10% from target allocation. Event-based: after a major market move (50%+ pump or dump). How CS portfolio rebalancing decisions work in practice — real case studies of what triggered moves and what happened after.

Tax-Loss Harvesting Within Rebalancing

Combine rebalancing with tax-loss harvesting: sell underperformers (crystallise loss), use proceeds to increase positions in winners. Net result: better portfolio AND lower tax bill. Timing: align rebalancing with EOFY for maximum tax benefit.

Sector Rotation Within Digital Assets

Early cycle: higher altcoin exposure, L1/L2 plays, emerging narratives. Mid cycle: shift toward established winners, reduce speculative positions. Late cycle: rotate to Bitcoin, increase stablecoins, reduce altcoin exposure. Bear market: maximum Bitcoin dominance, stablecoins for yield, minimal altcoin exposure.

TOPIC 3

Opportunity Cost Framework — The Real Cost of Holding

How to Calculate Opportunity Cost

"What else could this capital be doing?" If you're holding $10K in an altcoin that's down 40% with no catalyst — what would that $6K do if deployed into Bitcoin or a yield-bearing stablecoin? The calculation: current value of position × expected return of alternative = opportunity cost of holding.

Objective Criteria for Cut vs Hold Decisions

Has the thesis changed? (If yes → cut). Is there a clear catalyst in the next 3 months? (If no → cut). Would you buy this position today at current price? (If no → why are you holding?). Is it underperforming Bitcoin over 6 months? (If yes → reassess). The quarterly portfolio review: go through every position with these questions.

Systematic Portfolio Review Process

Quarterly review cadence. For each position: thesis check, performance vs Bitcoin, catalyst check, opportunity cost calculation. Output: hold, trim, add, or cut. Document the decision and rationale (for future reference). This process should be habitual — not emotional.

"My best investment decision wasn't Solana at 10x. It was getting out of Polygon before I lost 70-80%."

⚠️ TOPIC 4

Borrowing Against Holdings Instead of Selling — Advanced Strategy

The most advanced and potentially dangerous strategy in the OS. Powerful when used correctly — devastating when used without understanding liquidation risk. This topic requires extra care in delivery.

How Collateralised Lending Works

Deposit digital assets as collateral → receive a loan in fiat or stablecoins. You keep your Bitcoin (no sell = no CGT event). You get liquidity to use for other purposes. You pay interest on the loan. Example: deposit $100K BTC → borrow $50K USDC at 50% LTV.

Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratios and Interest Rates

LTV = loan amount ÷ collateral value. Conservative LTV: 25-30% (recommended). Moderate LTV: 40-50%. Aggressive LTV: 60%+ (dangerous). Interest rates: typically 5-12% APR depending on platform and LTV. Platform comparison: Ledn, Block Earner — how they work, fees, risks.

Tax Advantage: Borrowing Is Not a CGT Event

Key insight: selling Bitcoin = CGT event (potentially 23.5% tax on gains). Borrowing against Bitcoin = NOT a CGT event. You keep the asset, avoid the tax, and get liquidity. The interest cost of the loan may be less than the tax cost of selling. When the math works: long-term holders with large unrealised gains where the tax bill would be significant.

🚨 Margin Call and Liquidation Risk — The Danger Nobody Talks About

If the value of your collateral drops (Bitcoin price falls), your LTV increases. If LTV exceeds the liquidation threshold (typically 70-85%), the platform SELLS your collateral to repay the loan — without your permission. Example: borrow $50K against $100K BTC (50% LTV). BTC drops 40% → your collateral is now $60K → LTV is now 83% → LIQUIDATION. You lose your Bitcoin AND still owe any remaining balance. Stress-test scenario: what happens if BTC drops 30%, 50%, 70% while you have a loan?

When Borrowing Makes Sense vs When Selling Is Better

Borrowing makes sense: large unrealised gains (high tax cost to sell), strong conviction BTC will continue rising, conservative LTV (25-30%), ability to add collateral if price drops, short-term liquidity need. Selling is better: uncertain about price direction, can't afford to add collateral, need the money permanently, LTV would be >50%. The rule: if you don't understand liquidation, you shouldn't be borrowing.

"If you don't understand liquidation, you shouldn't be borrowing."

TOPIC 5

Market Cycle Exit Positioning — Selling at the Right Point

Historical Bitcoin Halving Cycle Data

2012 halving → 2013 peak: ~100x gain, peak ~12 months after halving. 2016 halving → 2017 peak: ~30x gain, peak ~18 months after halving. 2020 halving → 2021 peak: ~8x gain, peak ~18 months after halving. 2024 halving → 2025/26 peak: historically would suggest peak Q4 2025-Q2 2026. Diminishing returns pattern: each cycle's multiple is smaller. Not a prediction — a probability framework.

On-Chain Metrics for Cycle Positioning

NUPL (Net Unrealised Profit/Loss): when aggregate holders are in extreme profit → historically signals late cycle. MVRV (Market Value to Realised Value): above 3.5 = historically overheated. Puell Multiple: miner revenue relative to average — extreme highs signal cycle tops. How to read these charts and what levels to watch. Where to access: Glassnode, LookIntoBitcoin, CryptoQuant.

Ben's Framework for Identifying Cycle Peaks

Not exact timing — probability-based assessment. Multiple metrics must align (no single indicator is reliable). The "cluster of signals" approach: when 3+ metrics are in extreme territory simultaneously, probability of top increases. The CS approach: start trimming when metrics are elevated, increase selling as more signals align, never try to sell the exact top.

How to Build Cycle-Aware Sell Triggers

Written, objective, pre-committed rules. Example trigger set: "When NUPL > 0.75 AND MVRV > 3.0, begin selling 10% of altcoin positions per week." "When BTC is 18+ months post-halving AND showing extreme greed, trim 20% of BTC position." These triggers are set BEFORE the market reaches those levels — clear-headed planning, not emotional reaction.

CS Portfolio De-Risk Decisions as Case Studies

What triggered CS portfolio de-risk decisions in previous cycles. What on-chain data was showing at the time. What the team did and what happened after. Transparency: show the good calls AND the bad calls. The lesson: you don't need to be perfect — you need to be systematic and pre-committed.
